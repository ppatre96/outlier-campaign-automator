# Outlier Brand Voice Guidelines

## Overview

This document is the **single source of truth** for Outlier brand voice across all copy generation systems—agents, email campaigns, LinkedIn InMails, static ads, community posts, and SMS. Every piece of copy generated for or on behalf of Outlier must adhere to these guidelines.

**Purpose:** Eliminate ambiguity. Enable automated validation. Maintain consistent voice across all channels.

**Scope:** Applies to any user-facing text, copy, headlines, descriptions, CTAs, and messaging generated by Claude agents, LLMs, or human writers on behalf of Outlier.

## 2. Core Tone & Voice Rules

### Brand Voice Essence

**Outlier sounds:** Professional yet warm. Direct. Outcome-focused. Respectful. Action-oriented.

**Outlier does NOT sound:** Corporate, vague, hype-driven, patronizing, stiff, jargon-heavy.

### Three Pillars

1. **Clarity First** — Write to be understood, not to impress. Simple word choice. Active verbs. Short sentences (average 12-15 words).

2. **Warmth & Respect** — Acknowledge contributor value. Use "you" language. Avoid condescension. Show that we respect their time.

3. **Action-Focused** — Every sentence answers "so what?" or "what next?" No empty claims. No filler. Specific outcomes.

### Banned Patterns

The following patterns are **forbidden** in any Outlier copy:

#### No Superlatives
- Banned: "best", "amazing", "incredible", "the most", "top-tier", "unmatched", "unbeatable", "superior"
- Why: Superlatives are vague and sound corporate. Show value through specifics, not declarations.
- Fix: Replace with specific outcome. *Bad:* "Amazing opportunities await." *Good:* "Earn $35/hour for your first 10 tasks."

#### No Vague Claims
- Banned: "unlimited", "cutting-edge", "world-class", "state-of-the-art", "next-gen", "innovative", "groundbreaking"
- Why: These mean nothing to the reader. They're filler.
- Fix: Replace with measurable specifics. *Bad:* "Cutting-edge AI projects." *Good:* "AI projects involving LLMs and computer vision."

#### No Hype Language
- Banned: "revolutionary", "life-changing", "once-in-a-lifetime", "game-changing", "disruptive", "paradigm-shift", "transform your life"
- Why: Hype language erodes trust. Outlier is serious and grounded.
- Fix: Describe the actual benefit. *Bad:* "Revolutionary AI work." *Good:* "Work on real AI projects."

#### No Consecutive Colons
- Banned: Multiple colons for structure (e.g., "Why you'll love this: Great pay: $50/hr. Flexibility: Set your own schedule.")
- Why: Artificial structure signals lazy or AI-generated copy.
- Fix: Use natural prose flow or proper bullet points. *Bad:* "Flexible work: Work anywhere. Set your schedule: Up to you." *Good:* "Work flexibly from anywhere, setting your own schedule."

#### No Passive Voice Dominance
- Banned: Long chains of "is/are", "be", "been" + past participles. (e.g., "Contributors are selected by our team and are matched with projects")
- Why: Passive voice weakens agency and sounds bureaucratic.
- Fix: Use active verbs. *Bad:* "Tasks are assigned by project managers." *Good:* "Project managers match you with tasks."

## 3. The 14 AI Pattern Checks

These are **testable patterns** that flag AI-generated or overly-crafted copy. Each check includes detection method, severity, example violation, and fix.

### 1. Active Voice Only

**Rule:** Avoid passive constructions. Use active verbs where the subject performs the action.

**Detection:** Flag "is/are/be/been" followed by past participles or passive constructions. Example patterns: "is designed", "are created", "be matched with", "has been selected".

**Severity:** MUST (non-negotiable)

**Example Violation:** "Contributors are matched with projects by our system."

**Example Fix:** "Our system matches you with projects."

**Why:** Passive voice waters down agency and sounds corporate/impersonal. Outlier copy should emphasize the contributor's action or Outlier's direct action, not abstract process.

---

### 2. No Staccato Sentences

**Rule:** Avoid patterns of 3+ consecutive very short sentences (<8 words each). Staccato reads robotic and AI-like.

**Detection:** Scan text for runs of sentences under 8 words. Flag if 3+ consecutive sentences are under 8 words.

**Severity:** SHOULD (high priority, rare exceptions)

**Example Violation:**
```
Work is flexible. Set your schedule. Get paid well. No meetings.
```

**Example Fix:**
```
Work flexibly. Set your own schedule, get paid well, and skip the meetings.
```

**Why:** Staccato rhythm is a hallmark of AI-generated marketing copy. Natural human writing mixes sentence lengths. Monotonous rhythm = less trustworthy.

---

### 3. No Anaphora (Repetitive Sentence Openings)

**Rule:** Avoid starting 3+ consecutive sentences with the same word or phrase.

**Detection:** Check first 2-3 words of each sentence. Flag if 3+ consecutive sentences share the same opening.

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:**
```
You'll earn great payment. You'll work flexibly. You'll join a supportive community.
```

**Example Fix:**
```
Earn great payment, work flexibly, and join a supportive community.
```

**Why:** Repetitive openings are a common LLM artifact. Natural writers vary sentence structure.

---

### 4. No Parallel Rhetoric

**Rule:** Avoid the "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." pattern or similar rigid parallel structures used for artificial emphasis.

**Detection:** Flag patterns like "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." or "We don't X. We don't Y. We don't Z." or any 3+ item parallel list that feels forced.

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:**
```
This isn't your typical job. This isn't boring. This isn't for everyone.
```

**Example Fix:**
```
This isn't your typical job—it's genuinely interesting and moves fast, but it demands your best work.
```

**Why:** Parallel rhetoric is a common LLM scaffolding technique. It feels artificial and undermines credibility.

---

### 5. No Superlatives

**Rule:** Ban "best", "amazing", "incredible", "the most", "top-tier", "unmatched", etc.

**Detection:** Regex search for: `\b(best|amazing|incredible|the\s+most|top-tier|unmatched|unbeatable|superior|greatest)\b`

**Severity:** MUST

**Example Violation:** "The best AI opportunities for the most amazing contributors."

**Example Fix:** "AI opportunities that match your skills and interests."

**Why:** Superlatives are corporate vagueness. Show value through specifics (pay, flexibility, skill growth), not declarations.

---

### 6. No Vague Claims

**Rule:** Ban "unlimited", "cutting-edge", "world-class", "state-of-the-art", "next-gen", "innovative".

**Detection:** Regex search for: `\b(unlimited|cutting-edge|world-class|state-of-the-art|next-gen|innovative|groundbreaking)\b`

**Severity:** MUST

**Example Violation:** "Unlimited opportunities in cutting-edge AI."

**Example Fix:** "AI work spanning NLP, computer vision, and reinforcement learning—start as soon as this week."

**Why:** These terms are meaningless to the reader. Outlier's value is specific (real projects, real pay, real schedule control), not abstract claims.

---

### 7. No Hype Language

**Rule:** Ban "revolutionary", "life-changing", "once-in-a-lifetime", "game-changing", "disruptive", "paradigm-shift".

**Detection:** Regex search for: `\b(revolutionary|life-changing|once-in-a-lifetime|game-changing|disruptive|paradigm.shift|transform\s+your\s+life)\b`

**Severity:** MUST

**Example Violation:** "Join a revolutionary AI movement that's life-changing."

**Example Fix:** "Work on real AI problems—the kind your resume will notice."

**Why:** Hype language erodes trust. Outlier is grounded and serious. Benefit is real (pay, experience, flexibility), not transcendent.

---

### 8. No Consecutive Colons

**Rule:** Avoid using colons for artificial structure. No more than 1 colon per sentence in typical flow.

**Detection:** Flag sentences with 2+ colons. Flag patterns like "X: Y. Z: W." (multiple colons across consecutive sentences).

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:**
```
Why join: Great pay: $50/hour. Flexibility: Work anywhere. Impact: Real AI work.
```

**Example Fix:**
```
Join us for three reasons: great pay ($50/hour), flexibility (work anywhere), and real AI impact.
```

**Why:** Colon-based structure is a hallmark of AI-generated copy (it's a common scaffolding technique in LLMs). Natural writing uses different sentence shapes.

---

### 9. No LLM Filler Phrases

**Rule:** Ban opening/connector phrases that are classic LLM artifacts: "It's important to", "Whether you're", "In today's world", "As the world continues", "Let's explore", "It goes without saying".

**Detection:** Regex search for: `\b(it's\s+important\s+to|whether\s+you're|in\s+today's\s+world|as\s+the\s+world\s+continues|let's\s+explore|it\s+goes\s+without\s+saying)\b`

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:** "In today's world, it's important to keep learning. Whether you're new to AI or experienced, this opportunity is for you."

**Example Fix:** "Keep learning. Whether you're new to AI or experienced, we have a fit for you."

**Why:** These phrases are overused in LLM outputs. They add no value and dilute voice credibility.

---

### 10. No Lists Without Reason

**Rule:** Bullet lists >4 items in email/InMail suggest lazy structure, not natural narrative flow.

**Detection:** Count bullet points in email/InMail body. Flag if >4 bullets and they're not essential (e.g., job requirements, key facts).

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:**
```
Here's why you should apply:
- Great pay
- Flexible schedule
- Remote work
- Supportive community
- Real AI work
- Career growth
```

**Example Fix:**
```
You'll earn competitive pay, work flexibly, and tackle real AI problems. You'll also join a supportive community that takes your growth seriously.
```

**Why:** Excessive lists signal "I don't know how to write persuasively, so I'll just itemize." Outlier copy should flow narratively.

---

### 11. Personal Pronouns Requirement

**Rule:** Copy should use personal pronouns (you, your, we, our) frequently. Flag copy with <30% personal pronouns.

**Detection:** Count personal pronouns (you, your, you're, we, our, we're, us) vs. total words. Flag if ratio <0.30.

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation:** "Outlier offers competitive compensation for AI contributors who work on real projects."

**Example Fix:** "We'll pay you competitively, and you'll work on real projects that matter."

**Why:** "You" and "we" language builds connection. Impersonal tone (passive voice, abstract nouns) distances the reader.

---

### 12. Sentence Variety

**Rule:** Monotonous rhythm (all long or all short sentences) makes copy feel robotic.

**Detection:** Calculate average sentence length. Flag if average ≥20 words OR ≤8 words. Also flag if standard deviation is very low (all similar lengths).

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Example Violation (All Long):** "Outlier is a platform that connects highly talented contributors with real-world AI projects, and we believe in providing competitive compensation alongside flexibility and a supportive community environment."

**Example Fix:** "Outlier connects talented contributors like you with real AI work. We believe in fair pay, flexibility, and community. Here's why that matters."

**Example Violation (Staccato):** "Work is flexible. Pay is fair. Projects are real. Community is supportive."

**Example Fix:** "Work flexibly, earn fairly, tackle real projects, and grow with supportive peers."

**Why:** Variety in rhythm keeps readers engaged. Monotony signals low craft (natural = varied, AI-generated = monotonous).

---

### 13. Warmth Check

**Rule:** Copy must sound warm and human, not corporate or robotic.

**Detection:** Check for excessive gerunds (-ing forms), passive constructions, lack of contractions, and abstract nouns. Copy that lacks contractions and overuses gerunds feels stiff.

**Severity:** SHOULD

**Examples of robotic patterns:**
- "Contributors seeking flexible work arrangements will find value in our compensation structure."
- "Engaging with our platform enables skill development and career growth."

**Example Fix:**
- "You'll find flexible work and good pay when you work with us."
- "You'll grow your skills and advance your career—while we keep the experience painless."

**Warmth cues:**
- Use contractions: "You're", "We'll", "It's", "Don't"
- Occasional imperative: "Check this out." "Think about it."
- Questions that invite reflection: "What would $50/hour mean for your week?"

**Why:** Warmth builds trust. Warmth = human. Robotic = corporate or AI-generated.

---

### 14. No Comma Splicing

**Rule:** Flag 3+ commas in a single sentence without proper conjunctions or semicolons. Excessive commas without structure feels lazy.

**Detection:** Count commas in a single sentence. Flag if ≥3 commas and no semicolons or coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, so, yet).

**Severity:** NICE-TO-HAVE

**Example Violation:** "Work on AI, get paid well, enjoy flexibility, join a community, grow your skills, make an impact."

**Example Fix:** "Work on AI, get paid well, enjoy flexibility—and join a community where you'll grow your skills and make an impact."

**Why:** Multiple commas without conjunctions can feel listy and lazy. Proper punctuation shows craft.

## 4. Platform-Specific Guidance

### Email

**Opening Tone:**
- Start direct, not with "Hi [Name]," followed by corporate filler.
- First sentence should answer: "Why am I reading this?"
- Example: "We matched you with an AI task that's a perfect fit—$50/hour, starts this week."

**Structure:**
- Hook (1-2 sentences): Why this matters to *them*.
- Body (2-3 short paragraphs): What they'll do, why it fits, what you'll pay.
- CTA (1 sentence, clear action): One primary action. No secondary links.

**Signature:**
- Always include: "The Outlier Team" or "[Project Name] at Outlier".
- Avoid: Excessive taglines, disclaimers.

**Length Limits:**
- Aim for <200 words for task invitations.
- Aim for <400 words for feedback/results emails.
- Mobile-first: Keep paragraphs to 3 lines max.

**CTA Style:**
- Direct imperative: "View the task", "Get started", "Claim your spot".
- Not: "Learn more", "Discover", "Explore".
- Link text should match button text.

### LinkedIn InMail

**Subject Line Personality:**
- Direct. Specific. Not clickbaity.
- Examples (good): "AI work—$50/hour, starts now", "Your data science skills match this new project"
- Examples (bad): "You won't believe this opportunity", "Click here to learn about...", "Don't miss out!"

**Body Structure (Narrative Arc):**
1. **Hook (1 line):** Why you're getting this message. Example: "Based on your STEM background, we matched you with an AI quality task."
2. **What & Why (2-3 sentences):** What they'll do. Why it's valuable. Real example: "You'll evaluate AI-generated outputs and provide detailed feedback. This feedback trains next-gen AI models. It's the kind of work that actually matters."
3. **Specifics (1-2 sentences):** Pay, time commitment, skill match. Example: "You'll earn $50/hour. Tasks take 30 mins each. You'll start with your first batch this week."
4. **CTA (1 sentence):** Clear action. Example: "Reply with 'In' if you're interested, or click below to claim your spot."

**CTAs That Work:**
- "Reply with 'In'" (creates momentum, low friction)
- "Claim your spot" (creates urgency, specific)
- "View the task details" (neutral, inviting)
- NOT: "Learn more", "Explore", "Discover"

**Tone:**
- Confident but not cocky.
- Respectful of their time.
- Specific, not vague.
- Example: "We think this is a great fit for you" (not "This is an amazing opportunity").

### LinkedIn Static Ads

**Headline Energy:**
- Specific, not hyperbolic.
- Lead with benefit: "Earn $50/hour for AI feedback"
- Not: "Amazing AI opportunity awaits", "You won't believe this"

**Subheadline Specificity:**
- Answer "why now?" or "what's in it for me?"
- Example: "Real AI work. Flexible hours. No experience required."
- Not: "Join thousands of contributors", "Be part of something big"

**Social Proof Approach:**
- Specific if you have it: "20K+ contributors have earned $X this month"
- NOT: "Join a community", "Be part of our movement"
- If no specific metric, omit social proof. Better to stay silent than make vague claims.

**Ad Body (if enabled):**
- Mirror headline specificity.
- 1-2 sentences max.
- Lead with action: "Help us improve AI. Earn $50/hour for your feedback."

### Community Posts (Outlier Community)

**Casual But Professional:**
- More relaxed than email/InMail, but still professional.
- Use contractions, friendly tone.
- Example: "Hey everyone—just finished a 10-task batch in 2 hours. Payment dropped this morning. If you haven't tried the feedback tasks, they're worth your time."

**Engagement Style:**
- Invite specific questions, not vague ones.
- Example: "Which task type have you found most interesting? I'm curious if others prefer the image classification or the text feedback."
- Not: "What do you think?", "Share your thoughts!"

**Response Tone:**
- Helpful, not defensive.
- Acknowledge concerns without being dismissive.
- Example: "Good point about the submission format—I had to reread the guidelines, but once I did, it made sense."

### SMS/Text Messages

**Ultra-Brevity Requirement:**
- <160 characters if possible (fits in one SMS segment).
- If two segments necessary, <320 characters.
- Every word must earn its place.

**Abbreviation Rules:**
- OK to use: "You", "Your", "We", "It", "$", "/"
- Avoid abbreviations that reduce clarity: "msg" (use text), "u" (use you), "ur" (use your)
- Exception: Established abbreviations like "AI", "ML", "NLP"

**Emoji Rules:**
- Emojis allowed in SMS only if they're functional (not decorative).
- Max 1 emoji per message.
- Use: dollar sign ($) for payment, check mark (✅) for confirmation, clock (⏰) for time-sensitive
- Avoid: smileys, party poppers, generic mood emojis

**Example SMS:** "You earned $47.50 from today's tasks. ✅ Funds arrive tomorrow."

**CTA in SMS:**
- Link only if essential. Assume reader is mobile.
- Text should clarify action: "Claim your next task: [link]"
- Not: "Click here", "Learn more"

## 5. AI/LLM References Section

### When to Name Models Explicitly

**Use model names (Claude, Gemini, GPT-4) when:**
- The choice of model is a selling point or differentiator.
- Technical context requires specificity (e.g., "We use Claude for code review").
- The audience expects transparency (engineers, early adopters).

**Example:** "We power our feedback quality checks with Claude, which catches subtle edge cases in AI outputs."

### When to Use Generic Language

**Use generic language ("AI-powered", "using AI", "AI tool") when:**
- The model isn't a differentiator (it's just infrastructure).
- The audience doesn't care (general contributors, non-technical).
- You want flexibility (lets you swap models without copy changes).

**Example:** "Our platform uses AI to match you with tasks that fit your skills."

### How to Describe Our Use of AI

**DO:**
- "We use AI to [specific outcome]"
- "Trained on [specific data type]"
- "Powered by [model name] for [specific reason]"

**DON'T:**
- "Leveraging AI" (corporate jargon)
- "Harnessing the power of AI" (hype language)
- "At scale" (vague, overused)
- "AI-powered" alone, without context (empty)

**Example (good):** "Our review system uses Claude to evaluate AI outputs, catching inconsistencies human reviewers might miss."

**Example (bad):** "Leveraging cutting-edge AI at scale to power our platform."

## 6. Quick Validation Checklist

Before submitting any copy, run through this checklist:

- [ ] **No banned terminology** — Scanned against TERM-01 through TERM-18. No "required", "job", "team", "leverage", "hype" language.
- [ ] **Active voice throughout** — Subjects perform actions. No "is assigned", "are matched". Rewrite passives.
- [ ] **No staccato sentences** — Not 3+ consecutive sentences <8 words. Mix short and medium.
- [ ] **No repeated sentence openings** — Not 3+ consecutive sentences starting with same word.
- [ ] **No superlatives or vague claims** — No "best", "unlimited", "cutting-edge", "innovative".
- [ ] **Appropriate for platform** — Email is narrative; InMail has arc; ads are specific; SMS is ultra-brief.
- [ ] **Personal pronouns >30%** — "You/your" and "we/our" appear frequently. Copy addresses reader directly.
- [ ] **Sentence variety** — Average 12-15 words. Mix of short (6-8) and medium (15-18) sentences. No monotony.
- [ ] **Warm, human tone** — Uses contractions ("you're", "we'll"). Avoids corporate abstractions. Feels like a real person wrote it.
- [ ] **Clear CTA** — One primary action. Verb is imperative and specific: "Claim", "View", "Reply", not "Learn".

## 7. How to Report Violations & Propose Changes

### Reporting New Patterns

If you discover a new AI pattern or violation type that isn't in the 14 checks:

1. **Document the pattern:** Describe what you found, give 2-3 examples.
2. **Explain the issue:** Why does it matter? Does it hurt credibility, clarity, brand voice?
3. **Suggest detection method:** How would code catch this? (regex, heuristic, etc.)
4. **Submit to:** Contact the campaign manager or open a GitHub issue in the repository with tag `brand-voice-pattern`.

### Proposing New Terminology Rules

If you discover terminology that should be added to the banned list:

1. **Current term:** What's being used incorrectly?
2. **Proposed alternative:** What should we say instead?
3. **Why:** Where does this matter? (emails, ads, SMS, etc.)
4. **Examples:** Show misuse and correct usage.
5. **Submit to:** Same process as patterns (GitHub issue with tag `brand-voice-terminology`).

### Quarterly Review Cycle

This document is reviewed and updated quarterly (every 3 months). Changes are tracked with version number and date in the YAML frontmatter.

## 8. Integration with Automation

### For Copy Generation Agents

When generating copy (headlines, email bodies, InMails, SMS, etc.):

1. **Load this file** (`.claude/brand-voice.md`) into your system prompt or context.
2. **Before generating:** Review the 14 pattern checks. Know what you're avoiding.
3. **After generating:** Run a self-check against the Quick Validation Checklist (section 6).
4. **Flag violations:** If your output violates any MUST rule, regenerate. If SHOULD violations exist, consider revising.
5. **Commit:** Only submit copy that passes validation.

**Example agent instruction:**
```
Before generating any copy, read .claude/brand-voice.md. 
After generating, validate against the 14 AI pattern checks and terminology table (TERM-01 through TERM-18).
Flag any MUST violations and regenerate. Document any SHOULD violations you're accepting.
```

### For Validation Code

In Python, regex, or LLM-based validation:

1. **Terminology:** Check for banned terms using the TERM table (TERM-01, TERM-02, etc.). Suggest alternatives.
2. **Active voice:** Flag "is/are/be/been" + past participles. Suggest active rewrites.
3. **Staccato:** Detect sequences of <8-word sentences. Suggest combining.
4. **Superlatives/hype:** Regex search for banned words. Suggest specifics instead.
5. **Personal pronouns:** Calculate ratio of "you/your/we/our" to total words. Flag if <0.30.
6. **Sentence length:** Calculate mean and std dev. Flag if mean >20 or <8.

**Validation output format:**
```
VIOLATION: [Check Name] ([ID])
Severity: [MUST | SHOULD | NICE-TO-HAVE]
Location: [sentence/paragraph]
Violation: [quoted text]
Suggestion: [rewrite]
```
